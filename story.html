What getting locked out of Claude taught me about AI

I told myself I'd touch grass. Take the hint. Breathe. I didn't touch grass.

Cris Vinson Facilitator, AI Hour · June 2026

I got locked out of Claude.

Not frozen out by Anthropic. Not blocked for bad behavior. Just hit the weekly usage cap and got the message telling me to come back later. Which I knew was coming — I'd been burning through my limit faster and faster as my workflows got heavier. But knowing something is coming doesn't make it less annoying when it arrives.

I told myself I'd touch grass. Take the hint. Breathe.

I didn't touch grass.

The Detour

I had workflows running. Agents mid-build. A deadline I was pretending didn't exist. The last thing I wanted was a forced pause. So I did what any reasonable person does when their main tool goes down: I looked for a workaround.

That's how I found OpenClaw. And then Hermes. And then Odysseus — three open-source AI tools from a community that is genuinely hell-bent on making AI use accessible, affordable, and out from under any subscription limit.

OpenClaw held my attention for about twenty minutes, until I saw the first API bill. Twenty dollars. In one hour. Then came the security warnings. Then the setup complexity. Then the realization that "run it locally" meant "with a GPU you don't own."

So I kept Hermes and Odysseus open in other tabs and started poking at them, half-convinced I'd find my workaround. I didn't. But something else happened instead.

Using a completely different tool showed me something I couldn't see from inside Claude: my current workflow was small.

The Revelation

Not small in effort. Small in possibility.

I had been using Claude the way most people use a calculator — tap in a question, read the answer, close the tab. Prompt in, answer out. Start fresh every time. I had no idea what it could actually do when you stopped treating it like a search engine and started treating it like a collaborator.

Looking at OpenClaw's architecture — the way it handled multi-step tasks, the way it passed context between agents, the way it was designed to do things rather than just answer things — I could see the shape of what was possible. And then I looked at my own setup and realized: Claude could already do all of that. I just hadn't asked it to.

The lock-out wasn't punishment. It was perspective.

Going Deeper

I put OpenClaw down and picked Claude back up. But differently.

Instead of dabbling with alternatives, I decided to go deeper into what I already had. I learned multi-agent trees — how to chain Claude instances so they could hand work off to each other without waiting for me. I built headless workflows that ran without my hand-holding. I gave my agents hands and feet: the ability to browse, write files, trigger other processes, loop back with results.

The Claude I came back to wasn't a chat window. It was infrastructure.

That depth came with costs. The more I could do, the more compute I needed. I went from the $20 plan to the $100 plan to the $200 max plan in three months. Then I got downsized. The $200 plan had to go.

I dropped to $100. And with my usage where it was — multi-agent containers, heavy workflows, back-to-back tasks — that meant cooldowns. One to four hours, every other hour. My tool environment couldn't support my containers anymore and I was stuck waiting out the clock.

I needed a sidearm.

The Sidearm

That's when I came back around to the open-source world — not to replace Claude, but to understand it properly. I ran OpenClaw, Hermes, and Odysseus locally during cooldown windows. My MacBook Pro, 32 GB of RAM, the machine I trusted with everything, hit 35 GB out of 36 GB during the heavier local model runs. The fan sounded like it was filing a formal complaint.

But I learned three things that actually mattered:

  • 01 The open-source AI community is genuinely building toward accessibility. Running local models is not a hobbyist project anymore — setting this up may be the next hands-on IT service. The community is moving fast.
  • 02 Local models minimize your API costs and your environmental footprint. Theoretically. In practice, the compute cost just moves from cloud servers to your own hardware. The footprint shifts; it doesn't disappear.
  • 03 It burns through resources. My 32 GB machine was hitting 35 GB of memory usage. Local AI is not a lightweight backup option — it's a different kind of investment.

Knowing all of that, I made my peace with the limits. I use the cooldown hours to think instead of build. And I keep a local model running as a sidearm — for small tasks, fast lookups, the things that don't need Claude's full capability. The two tools don't compete. They cover different jobs.

Most non-tech founders are using Claude the way I was using it before the lock-out. One prompt at a time. Closing the tab and wondering if this AI thing is really as big as everyone says.

What This Means For You

It is. But you won't see it from inside the chat window.

The constraint that locked me out also showed me the ceiling I was operating under. I had been getting value from Claude — real value — but I had no idea there was a whole other floor above me. The only reason I found it was because something forced me to look up.

The workshop on June 13 is the perspective I couldn't give myself.

In four hours, you will ship something real — not a draft, not a plan, an actual page that lives on the internet. You will automate one task that currently drains your week. And you will leave with a Claude setup that feels less like a tool you use and more like a working relationship you can build on.

That is the other side of the wall. You don't have to wait for a lock-out to find it.
